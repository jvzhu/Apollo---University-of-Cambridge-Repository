# Research Notes: Native-Speaker Gatekeeping — Hong Kong NCS Policy and Figurative Language Access

## The connection

Two policy/research contexts, structurally mirrored:

1. **FigurativeAccess (Cambridge)** — treats *native-speaker intuition* as the ground-truth standard for identifying and interpreting figurative language in English. Annotators must be native speakers of British English; the working assumption is that native competence gives privileged, non-substitutable access to figurative meaning that non-native speakers (and, per the project's premise, current AI models) lack.

2. **Hong Kong NCS policy** — support infrastructure (Induction Programme, Initiation Programme, curriculum development support, placement assistance) is organized around a **deficit model**: Non-Chinese Speaking students are defined by what they lack relative to a native-Cantonese/native-Chinese-literacy norm, and the system's job is to move them toward that norm, primarily through remedial/compensatory scaffolding rather than treating multilingual competence as a resource in its own right.

Both frameworks take "nativeness" as the reference point against which access to language — literal, figurative, or academic — is measured. Where they diverge is in what nativeness is taken to *license*:

- In FigurativeAccess, nativeness licenses **authority to judge** (whose intuitions count as data).
- In the HK NCS framework, non-nativeness licenses **eligibility for support** (whose needs count as requiring intervention).

## Open questions worth developing

- **Whose figurative competence goes unmeasured?** The NCS population — students acquiring Chinese as an additional language in a Cantonese-medium system — would, by the FigurativeAccess protocol's own logic, be excluded as annotators of Chinese figurative language, yet are exactly the population whose *comprehension* of figurative Chinese (idioms, chengyu, classroom register) is the site of active pedagogical intervention. There's an asymmetry: non-native figurative competence is treated as a research/annotation liability in one context and as the entire object of remediation in the other — never as a data source about how figurative meaning is actually acquired or negotiated cross-linguistically.

- **Native-speaker intuition as methodological convenience vs. epistemic claim.** Worth interrogating (this connects to your broader throughline about external interpretive frames producing the categories they claim only to describe): does "native British English speaker" as an inclusion criterion encode a claim about where figurative meaning *resides* (in a speech community's shared intuition), or is it simply the most tractable proxy available for corpus annotation purposes? The Ding/Zhang Zhupo pattern in your *Jin Ping Mei* work — an interpretive frame imported from outside a text that ends up constituting the categories it claims to find — has a loose structural echo here: "native speaker" as a category is doing similar constitutive work for what counts as figurative *at all*.

- **Comparative angle**: Hong Kong's approach (deficit/remediation-oriented, individual-student-focused) vs. other jurisdictions' NCS/EAL frameworks that explicitly build multilingual metalinguistic awareness into curriculum (e.g., some Canadian and Scandinavian models treat cross-linguistic transfer as an asset for exactly the kind of figurative/pragmatic competence FigurativeAccess is trying to isolate as a "native" trait). Could be worth a comparison table if this becomes a fuller piece.
