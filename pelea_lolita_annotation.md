# Annotation: Pelea on Lolita Subculture — Possible Citation

**Source**: Pelea, Crînguţa-Irina. "Japan's Fashion Subculture: Lolita, From Cuteness to Feminist Revolution and Escapism." In *Storytelling in Luxury Fashion*, 137–153. Routledge, 2020. DOI: 10.4324/9781003022794-11.

## Relevant claim (from abstract only — chapter itself is paywalled)

Pelea argues that Lolita fashion functions as a "semiotic matrix" where Japanese postmodern society, appropriated Victorian/Edwardian aristocratic dress, and digital storytelling (sixty Japanese-language Lolita blogs) intertwine into a single coherent narrative system — one she describes as saturated with nostalgic, metaphoric tales of a past that never belonged to Japan in the first place.

## Point of contact with your framework

This is a useful supporting case for the *ma* 間 / movement-hermeneutics argument in *Exploring Art, Knowledge and Movement in Japanese Fashion*: Lolita's appropriated Victorian heritage is itself an *invented* archive — the "past" it nostalgically cites was never Japan's own, which makes the constitutive (rather than descriptive) work of the fashion-as-archive frame even more visible than in the Kawakubo/avant-garde case. Where your argument treats avant-garde garment construction as unconsciously encoding real archival material (court drama records, palaeography), Pelea's Lolita case is the inverse and arguably sharper example: a fashion subculture openly manufacturing a heritage narrative it doesn't historically possess, then treating that manufactured narrative as authentic origin.

Worth flagging as a comparison rather than a direct source, since Pelea's methodology (netnography, blog content analysis) is sociological rather than art-historical/hermeneutic, and her focus (contemporary subculture, digital storytelling) sits outside your archival timeframe (1980s designers, Kyoto/Tokyo museum archives). But as a citation, it would sharpen the claim that heritage-as-frame is doing constitutive work — even in a case where the "heritage" is acknowledged fiction.
