# Annotation: Cliffe on Kimono as Living Fashion System — Possible Citation

**Source**: Cliffe, Sheila. *The Social Life of Kimono: Japanese Fashion Past and Present*. London: Bloomsbury Academic, 2018. DOI: 10.5040/9781474286107.

## Relevant claim

Cliffe's material-culture study argues against treating the kimono as a static, "rigidly traditional" national costume — a framing that comes from outside the garment and gets projected onto it as if descriptive. She reconstructs the kimono instead as an active fashion system: something that changes meaning through wearers, producers, advertising, and social media, and that has always operated on the same terms as any other fashion system (contra the assumption, per Times Higher Education's review of the book, that fashion theory is a Western-exclusive category). Her second, more polemical aim is precisely to unseat the assumption that only Paris/the West produces "fashion" while non-Western garments are relegated to timeless "tradition" or "costume."

## Point of contact with your framework

This is close to the strongest possible parallel case for the *ma* 間 argument in *Exploring Art, Knowledge and Movement in Japanese Fashion*: Cliffe's target is the same interpretive move you're diagnosing — an external frame ("traditional costume," "unchanging national dress") that claims only to describe the kimono but in fact produces its perceived fixity, foreclosing the garment's actual historical variability. Where your book traces how avant-garde designers' garment construction encodes and transmits archival/somatic memory, Cliffe's case shows the inverse mechanism at work on a different garment: an interpretive frame (the "tradition" label) suppressing the archive of change that the kimono's actual history contains.

Also directly useful for situating your work's disciplinary intervention: Cliffe's argument that fashion theory shouldn't be treated as exclusively Western gives you an explicit precedent to cite when framing the *ma* concept's status as a non-Western contribution to fashion theory generally, not just a regional case study to be read through Western frameworks.

## Suggested use
Likely most useful in framing/introduction material — as a citation establishing precedent for reading Japanese dress as an active, meaning-producing system rather than passive ethnographic subject — before pivoting to your book's more specific archival/hermeneutic claims about the 1980s avant-garde.

## Citation network (via ResearchGate, 17 citing works total)

A few citing works sharpen or extend the same tradition/fashion binary Cliffe is challenging — worth tracking down directly rather than relying on Cliffe alone:

- **Ian Fookes**, "Yuki Kihara's 'A Song about Samoa': Reimagining the Pacific through Japanese Relations" (2022). Cites Cliffe specifically to name the binary at stake: western fashion theory treats "traditional" dress as categorically opposed to "fashionable" clothing, with non-Western styles assumed static and craft-produced rather than industrially fashion-driven. This is close to a direct statement of the frame your *ma* 間 argument is also diagnosing — worth citing alongside Cliffe as a second source naming the same binary in a different (Pacific/Japanese-diasporic) context.

- **Lucile Druet** — three separate pieces citing Cliffe: "Fashionable pilgrims: Rental and second-hand kimono shops styling paths of new embodied communities" (2021); "New fashion 'orientations': the tailoring of kimono in Parisian, African, and Indian dress designs" (2023); "Dressed by Nature, Adorned by Design: Seeing and reading the Seasons with Kimono" (2024). Druet's throughline — kimono as simultaneously "staying the same and evolving," a fixed silhouette read through variable symbols/patterns/accessories — offers a formal vocabulary (fixed structure + variable surface reading) that maps onto how your book treats garment construction as encoding variable historical/somatic content within a stable material form.

- **Julie Valk** — "The smell of Shōwa: Time, materiality and regimes of value in Japan's second-hand kimono industry" (2020) and "Cultural Literacy as Mission and Product: Age, Class, and Gender in Japan's Kimono Academies" (2024); also author of the monograph *Selling the Kimono: An Ethnography of Crisis, Creativity and Hope* (Routledge, 2021). Valk's focus on kimono as a site where value, time, and materiality intersect is the closest of the three to your book's "physical archive" claim — worth checking whether *Selling the Kimono* treats garments as archives of somatic/temporal memory in a way that either supports or needs distinguishing from your movement hermeneutics framework.
